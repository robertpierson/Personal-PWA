Foundation models and the pretrain-then-adapt paradigm

Lesson 1

What makes a model "foundational"

In 2013, word2vec showed that a shallow network trained to predict neighboring words from a huge unlabeled corpus produced vectors useful for almost any downstream text task — analogy solving, clustering, classification — without ever being told what those tasks were. It was a single, fixed set of embeddings reused everywhere. Five years later, ELMo (2018) showed the vectors could depend on context — the same word gets a different vector in different sentences — by running a full bidirectional LSTM language model and reading off its internal states. Later that year and into 2019, BERT and GPT replaced the LSTM with the transformer (Course 2 covers the mechanism; here you only need to know it scaled better) and showed that a single pretrained network, lightly adapted, could top the leaderboard on nearly every standard NLP benchmark simultaneously. None of this was named at the time. It was just "pretraining," a step bolted onto whatever task you actually cared about.

The name arrived in 2021, when a Stanford group (the Center for Research on Foundation Models, CRFM) published a report proposing foundation model as the umbrella term for this whole family: models trained on broad data at scale, adaptable to a wide range of downstream tasks. The term was immediately controversial — critics pointed out that it renamed a practice (transfer learning from large pretrained networks) that NLP researchers had already been doing for three years, and that "foundation" implied a solidity and safety the models hadn't earned. That criticism is worth taking seriously rather than dismissing as academic turf war: a good chunk of what makes "foundation model" feel like a new category is branding for a trend already well underway. The useful part of the term isn't that it describes something new — it's that it gives a name to a cluster of properties that previously got treated as separate observations about separate models.

The three ingredients

Strip away the branding debate and three ingredients recur across every model people are willing to call foundational. The first is scale — not just parameter count, but scale across data, compute, and (usually) model size together, well beyond what any single downstream task could justify collecting or training for. The second is self-supervision: the training objective is derived automatically from the raw data itself, with no human-authored labels (Lesson 3 unpacks this in depth). The third, and the one that actually matters for calling something "foundational" rather than just "big," is generality of the resulting representation — the internal vectors the network produces have to be useful for tasks the model never saw during training, not just the one objective it was optimized for. A large model trained end-to-end on a single narrow task, however many GPU-hours it burns, is not a foundation model by this definition. A protein network trained purely to output a Rosetta energy score is not a foundation model. A protein network trained to reconstruct masked residues, whose intermediate representations turn out to encode secondary structure, contact maps, and remote homology all at once, is one.

This generality is what let a single family of pretrained protein language models — the ESM lineage (ESM-1, ESM-1b, ESM-2, trained by Meta AI on sequences from UniRef) — feed into structure prediction, function annotation, mutational-effect scoring, and design, all from encoders that were only ever trained to fill in masked amino acids. The pretraining objective (Lesson 3) never mentions structure. Structure just falls out of the representation because predicting a masked residue well requires implicitly modeling which residues are folded near which others.

Homogenisation: benefit and risk in the same fact

Because one backbone now feeds dozens of downstream systems, the field has undergone what the CRFM report calls homogenisation: instead of a hundred labs each building bespoke architectures for antibody design, enzyme classification, and stability prediction, most of them now start from the same handful of pretrained checkpoints (an ESM-2 checkpoint, say) and adapt from there. The benefit is real and large — adaptation is cheap (Lesson 2), quality is high, and progress on the backbone lifts every downstream task at once. The risk is the same fact viewed from the other side: a systematic blind spot or bias in the pretrained representation — say, underrepresentation of sequences from a particular taxonomic clade in UniRef because that clade is undersequenced — now propagates into every downstream model built on top of it, simultaneously, rather than being an isolated failure of one team's design choice. Homogenisation concentrates both capability and failure mode in the same object.

Emergent abilities — the claim, and the catch

A frequently repeated claim about foundation models at sufficient scale is that they display emergent abilities: capabilities absent or near-chance in smaller models that appear sharply, not gradually, once a model crosses some scale threshold — the qualitative jump looks less like a smooth curve and more like a phase transition. Schaeffer, Miller, and Re (2023) offered an important correction: much of the apparent sharpness is an artifact of the metric used to score the task, not a property of the underlying model. A metric like exact-match accuracy on a multi-step task is necessarily near zero until the model gets every step right, then rises — that's a property of multiplying several continuously-improving per-step probabilities together and thresholding the product, not evidence of a discontinuity in what the model learned. Switch to a continuous, per-token metric on the same task and model checkpoints, and the same capability often improves smoothly with scale all along. The honest position is that some abilities do genuinely require scale to reach useful accuracy — but "sharp and unpredictable" is often a measurement choice, not a law of nature, and you should always ask what metric produced an emergence claim before trusting it.

Old pipeline vs. new pipeline

The organizing shift is easiest to see by contrasting the shape of the two pipelines directly, in the protein domain.

# OLD: one model per task, trained from scratch each time
def train_stability_model(labelled_seqs, labels):
    model = RandomInitNetwork()
    train_from_scratch(model, labelled_seqs, labels)   # needs enough labels
    return model                                        # useless for any other task

def train_function_model(labelled_seqs, labels):
    model = RandomInitNetwork()                          # different network,
    train_from_scratch(model, labelled_seqs, labels)     # different labels,
    return model                                         # zero reuse from above

stability_model = train_stability_model(stability_seqs, stability_labels)
function_model  = train_function_model(function_seqs, function_labels)
# every new task = new architecture search + new from-scratch training run


# NEW: one backbone, many heads
backbone = pretrain_on_uniref50(objective="masked_residue_prediction")  # once, expensive
backbone.freeze()

def adapt(task_labelled_seqs, task_labels):
    embeddings = [backbone.embed(s) for s in task_labelled_seqs]  # cheap, reused code
    head = SmallHead()
    head.fit(embeddings, task_labels)          # trains in minutes, not weeks
    return head

stability_head = adapt(stability_seqs, stability_labels)
function_head  = adapt(function_seqs, function_labels)
contact_head   = adapt(contact_seqs, contact_labels)
# same frozen backbone underneath all three — the expensive step happened once

The old pipeline repeats the expensive step (from-scratch training) for every task. The new pipeline pays the expensive step (pretraining) exactly once and repeats only the cheap step (fitting a small head on frozen embeddings) — this is the asymmetry Lesson 2 quantifies in GPU-hours.

Key takeaways

  • "Foundation model" (Stanford CRFM, 2021) named a practice already three years old — critics rightly note the term is partly branding, but it does name a real, useful cluster of properties.
  • The three defining ingredients are scale, self-supervision, and generality of representation — not parameter count alone. Generality is the property that actually earns the name.
  • Homogenisation is the same fact viewed twice: shared backbones make adaptation cheap and lift every downstream task at once, and they propagate any shared blind spot into every downstream system at once.
  • Emergent abilities are real in some cases but Schaeffer et al. (2023) showed many "sharp" emergence curves are artifacts of a discontinuous metric (like exact-match) rather than a discontinuity in the model.
  • The ESM protein-language-model lineage is foundational precisely because a masked-residue objective — never mentioning structure — produces representations useful for structure, function, and design alike.

Lesson 2

The pretrain-then-adapt pipeline

The pretrain-then-adapt paradigm is a two-phase workflow: train one large model once on a broad, unlabeled corpus with a self-supervised objective (pretraining), then reuse that trained model, cheaply and repeatedly, to solve many specific downstream tasks (adaptation). Everything in this lesson is about what happens inside each phase and why splitting the work this way changed how the field allocates its effort.

Stage 1 — corpus curation

Pretraining starts with assembling the broadest defensible corpus, not the most task-relevant one. For protein language models, that corpus is typically UniRef50: a clustering of UniProt sequences at 50% identity, containing roughly 250 million representative sequences spanning essentially every organism that has ever been sequenced. Curation choices here matter more than they look: how you deduplicate near-identical sequences, whether you filter fragments or low-complexity regions, and how you weight overrepresented families (a handful of well-studied protein families dominate raw sequence counts) all shape what the pretrained model ends up good at. Corpus curation for pretraining is much closer to database engineering than to dataset labeling — there are no labels here to check for correctness, only completeness and duplication to manage at the scale of hundreds of millions of records.

Stage 2 — objective choice

Once the corpus exists, you need a self-supervised task the model can practice at scale. Unit 4 owns the mechanics of these objectives in detail; for now, just note that the choice of objective is a design decision made once, up front, and it fixes what kind of representation the model is capable of learning — a masked-reconstruction objective on individual residues learns something different from a whole-sequence contrastive objective, even on the identical corpus.

Stage 3 — the pretraining run

This is the expensive part: optimizing a large network (hundreds of millions to tens of billions of parameters, for the current ESM-2 family) against the chosen objective over the full corpus, for as long as compute budget allows. This is where every technique from Course 1 — the Adam-family optimizers, learning-rate schedules, batch normalization variants, gradient clipping, mixed-precision training — gets deployed at its most demanding, sustained scale: runs measured in millions of GPU-hours, spread across weeks on large clusters. Nothing about the run itself is new in kind; it is familiar training practice pushed to an extreme regime.

Stage 4 — the checkpoint as artifact

The output of stage 3 is a checkpoint: a frozen set of weights, versioned and distributed like any other software artifact (ESM-2 checkpoints are published with explicit parameter counts — 8M, 35M, 150M, 650M, 3B, 15B — so a downstream user can trade quality for compute cost). Treating the checkpoint as a reusable artifact rather than a one-off experimental result is the pivot point of the whole paradigm: it is the thing that gets adapted, cited, benchmarked, and built on top of, independent of whoever trained it or why.

Stage 5 — the adaptation menu

Given a checkpoint, a downstream user picks from a menu of adaptation strategies, ordered here roughly from cheapest to most involved. Linear probing (also called feature extraction) freezes the entire pretrained network, runs data through it to get fixed embeddings, and trains only a small linear or shallow classifier on top — this is the code example below. Full fine-tuning unfreezes some or all of the pretrained weights and continues training them, at a much lower learning rate, on the downstream task's labeled data — more expensive, often more accurate, and riskier because it can distort or overwrite what pretraining learned (a failure mode called catastrophic forgetting). Prompting reframes the downstream task as an input the frozen model can answer directly, without any weight updates at all — Course 3 covers this in the language-model setting, and the same idea has protein analogues (querying a model with a partial sequence and asking it to complete or score the rest). Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT — adapters, LoRA, and similar methods, detailed in a later unit) inserts a small number of new trainable parameters into an otherwise frozen network, capturing most of full fine-tuning's benefit at a fraction of its memory and compute cost.

The economics, stated plainly

Here is the number that reorganized the field: a single pretraining run costs on the order of millions of GPU-hours, paid once, by whoever trains the checkpoint. A single adaptation — a linear probe, a small fine-tune, a handful of prompts — costs hours to at most a few GPU-days, paid by whoever wants to solve one specific task. That asymmetry is not a minor efficiency gain; it inverts who can participate in state-of-the-art modeling. Before this paradigm, a lab studying, say, enzyme thermostability needed enough labeled data and enough compute to train a competitive model from scratch. After it, that lab downloads a public ESM-2 checkpoint, embeds a few hundred labeled sequences, and trains a logistic head over a lunch break — with representation quality inherited from a pretraining run that cost orders of magnitude more than the lab could ever have afforded on its own. The asymmetry is also why checkpoints get published and cited as first-class research contributions in their own right, separate from any single downstream result: the expensive, hard-to-reproduce part of the work is stage 3, and everything after it is comparatively cheap and fast to iterate on.

# Feature extraction: frozen encoder, small head, 400 labelled examples
# Downstream task: does this sequence contain a signal peptide? (binary)

encoder = load_pretrained_checkpoint("esm2_t12_35M")   # frozen, no grad
encoder.eval()
for p in encoder.parameters():
    p.requires_grad = False

def embed_sequence(seq: str) -> "vector[35M-dim -> pooled 480-dim]":
    tokens = tokenize(seq)                    # (1, L)
    with no_grad():
        hidden = encoder(tokens)              # (1, L, 480)
        pooled = hidden.mean(dim=1)           # (1, 480)  mean over residues
    return pooled.squeeze(0)                  # (480,)

# Do this ONCE, cache the result — this is the expensive-looking step
# that is actually cheap because the encoder never trains here.
labelled_seqs, labels = load_labelled_set(n=400)      # 400 sequences, 0/1 labels
cached_vectors = [embed_sequence(s) for s in labelled_seqs]   # (400, 480)

# The only thing that trains: a logistic regression head
head = LogisticRegression(in_dim=480)
head.fit(cached_vectors, labels)              # converges in seconds on a laptop CPU

# Inference on a new, unlabelled sequence
new_vec = embed_sequence(new_sequence)
prediction = head.predict(new_vec)            # signal peptide: yes / no

The encoder runs 400 times total, ever — one forward pass per training example, cached forever after. Only the 480-parameter logistic head ever receives a gradient; the 35M-parameter encoder's weights never move.

Key takeaways

  • The pipeline has five stages: corpus curation, objective choice, the pretraining run, the checkpoint as artifact, and adaptation — the first three happen once, adaptation happens over and over by different users.
  • UniRef50's ~250M sequences illustrate corpus curation at pretraining scale: the work is deduplication and coverage, not labeling.
  • The adaptation menu runs cheapest-to-priciest: linear probe → full fine-tune → prompting → PEFT each trade compute cost against accuracy and forgetting risk differently.
  • The economic asymmetry — millions of GPU-hours once vs. hours per adaptation — is why checkpoints, not individual downstream results, became the field's primary reusable artifact.
  • In frozen feature extraction, the pretrained encoder never receives a gradient; only the small head trains, which is what makes 400 labelled examples enough.

Lesson 3

Self-supervision: where labels come from when there are none

UniRef50 holds roughly 250 million protein sequences. The Protein Data Bank — the entire accumulated output of decades of X-ray crystallography, NMR, and cryo-EM — holds on the order of 200,000 experimentally solved structures. Even generously assuming every solved structure maps to a distinct sequence in UniRef50, that is under 0.1% of the corpus carrying the label ("this sequence folds into this 3D structure") that downstream users actually want. Ordinary supervised learning needs (input, label) pairs; if you tried to train a structure-aware representation supervised, on labels alone, you would be training on a rounding error's worth of data and throwing away 99.9%+ of everything known about protein sequences. Supervised learning is not merely suboptimal here — it is dead on arrival. Self-supervision exists to solve exactly this ratio.

The pretext-task idea

A pretext task is a task manufactured from the raw data itself, with no external annotation, whose label is derived by a fixed, automatic rule rather than by a human expert. The recipe: take an unlabeled example, apply some corruption or transformation to it according to a rule you chose, and use the pre-corruption content (or the transformation itself) as the label. Nobody had to look at a single protein and decide anything about it. Every one of the 250 million UniRef50 sequences can generate its own pretext-task examples, for free, in parallel, forever — which is exactly the property that makes this compatible with corpora that outrun any conceivable labeling budget by six orders of magnitude.

What makes a pretext task good

Not every corruption rule produces a useful representation, and the difference matters more than it first appears. A good pretext task has to be hard enough to force the model to learn real structure to solve it — reconstructing a masked residue from its surrounding context requires implicitly modeling local secondary-structure propensities, and reconstructing several masked residues that are far apart in sequence but close in 3D space (as they often are, in a folded protein) requires implicitly modeling long-range contacts. A task that's too easy teaches nothing worth transferring. A task that's impossible teaches nothing either — there is no signal to learn from, only noise. The sweet spot is a task solvable only by building an internal model of the thing you actually care about (fold geometry, evolutionary constraint, chemical plausibility) as a side effect of getting better at the manufactured task.

Shortcut learning: the failure mode this creates

Shortcut learning is what happens when a pretext task admits a solution that doesn't require the real structure at all — some superficial statistical regularity in the corpus lets the model solve the pretext task while learning something far shallower than intended. A concrete protein-domain example: if masked residues were always chosen uniformly at random and the corpus contains many near-duplicate sequences (a risk UniRef's clustering at 50% identity is specifically designed to reduce, per Lesson 2's corpus-curation stage), a model could partly solve masked-residue prediction by recognizing which near-duplicate family a sequence belongs to and copying the typical residue at that position, rather than reasoning from local chemical or structural context at all. The pretext task's reported loss looks fine; the representation it produces transfers poorly to genuinely novel sequences. Shortcut learning is why corpus curation (deduplication, controlled diversity) and pretext-task design have to be considered together, not separately — a well-designed objective on a poorly curated corpus can still fail.

Judging representation quality without a downstream task

Because the whole point of self-supervised pretraining is to produce a representation before you know every downstream task it will be asked to serve, you need ways to judge representation quality that don't depend on any one specific labeled task. Two standard tools do this. A linear probe — the same technique as Lesson 2's feature-extraction adaptation, repurposed here as a diagnostic — trains a single linear layer on top of frozen embeddings for some small proxy labeled set (say, a few hundred structures) and reports accuracy; because the probe itself has almost no capacity to learn anything, high accuracy means the useful information was already linearly present in the frozen embedding, not constructed by the probe. A k-NN probe skips training entirely: embed a labeled reference set, embed a query sequence, and check whether the query's nearest neighbors by embedding distance share its label — if sequences with similar fold or function cluster near each other in the embedding space without ever being told to, that is direct evidence the space is organized the way you want, using nothing but distance. Both tools let you compare pretraining runs, corpora, and objectives against each other cheaply, well before committing to any expensive full adaptation.

import random

AMINO_ACIDS = "ACDEFGHIKLMNPQRSTVWY"  # 20 standard residue codes
MASK_TOKEN = ""

def mask_sequence(sequence: str, mask_prob: float = 0.15, seed=None):
    """
    Self-supervised pretext task: corrupt ~15% of residues, and return
    the corrupted sequence alongside a label built from the ORIGINAL
    sequence — the label costs nothing because we already had it.
    """
    rng = random.Random(seed)
    corrupted = list(sequence)
    targets = {}  # position -> true residue, only for masked positions

    for i, residue in enumerate(sequence):
        if rng.random() < mask_prob:
            targets[i] = residue          # the free label: what was there
            corrupted[i] = MASK_TOKEN     # the corruption: hide it

    return "".join(corrupted), targets

# Example
seq = "MKTAYIAKQRQISFVKSHFSRQLEERLGLIEVQAPILSRVGDGTQDNLSGAEKAVQVKVKALPDAQ"
corrupted_input, target = mask_sequence(seq, mask_prob=0.15, seed=7)
print(corrupted_input[:20])   # e.g. "MKTAYIAQRQISFV"
print(target)                 # e.g. {7: 'K', ...}  — positions & true residues

The label (targets) is read directly off the input before corruption — no human ever annotated this sequence. The model's job during pretraining is to predict each masked residue from the unmasked context around it.

Key takeaways

  • UniRef50's ~250M sequences vs. the PDB's ~200k solved structures is the concrete ratio that makes supervised learning infeasible and self-supervision necessary.
  • A pretext task manufactures its own label from the input by a fixed corruption rule — no human annotation, applicable to every sequence in the corpus for free.
  • A good pretext task must be hard enough to force real structure to be learned — too easy or too random teaches nothing transferable.
  • Shortcut learning happens when a superficial regularity (like near-duplicate sequences) lets the model solve the pretext task without learning the intended structure — a reason corpus curation and objective design are linked, not separate concerns.
  • Linear probes and k-NN probes judge representation quality using only a small proxy label set or none at all, letting you compare pretraining choices before committing to full downstream adaptation.

Question 1 Multiple choice

Which of the following is the property that most specifically distinguishes a "foundation model" from merely "a large model trained on a lot of data"?

  1. It has more parameters than any prior model in its domain.
  2. Its training run required more GPU-hours than any prior model.
  3. Its internal representation is general enough to be useful for downstream tasks it never saw during training.
  4. It was trained using a transformer architecture rather than an LSTM.
Show answer

Answer: C. Scale and self-supervision are common ingredients, but the property that actually earns the name "foundational" is that the representation transfers to tasks the model was never trained on — a large model optimized end-to-end for one narrow task fails this even at huge scale.

A and B describe scale alone, which Lesson 1 explicitly says is necessary but not sufficient — a large single-task model isn't foundational. D names an architecture choice (Course 2's topic), not a defining property; ELMo used an LSTM and is still considered part of this lineage.

Question 2 True / false

The term "foundation model" was coined by a 2021 Stanford (CRFM) report to describe a genuinely new training technique that did not exist before that publication.

  1. True
  2. False

The tempting reading is that a named term implies a new invention; Lesson 1 states directly that this criticism is worth taking seriously rather than dismissed.

Question 3 Fill in the blank

Schaeffer et al. (2023) argued that many claimed "emergent abilities" of large models are actually artifacts of using a   metric (like exact-match accuracy), and that switching to a continuous, per-step metric on the same models often reveals smooth improvement instead of a sharp jump.

Show answer

Answer: discontinuous (or nonlinear/thresholding) metric. A metric that requires every step of a multi-step task to be correct simultaneously will look like near-zero performance until per-step accuracy is high enough that the product of probabilities crosses a threshold — that's a property of the scoring rule, not necessarily of the underlying model's learning curve.

It is tempting to fill the blank with something like "a scale-based" or "a compute-based" metric — but the critique is specifically about the shape of the scoring function (discontinuous vs. continuous), not about what resource is being measured.

Question 5 Multiple choice

A lab freezes a pretrained ESM-2 encoder, embeds 400 labelled sequences once, caches the vectors, and trains only a logistic regression head on those vectors. Which adaptation strategy from Lesson 2's menu is this?

  1. Full fine-tuning
  2. Prompting

Answer: D. This is the defining pattern of linear probing: the encoder's weights are frozen and never receive a gradient; only a small classifier trained on top of cached, fixed embeddings updates.

Question 7 Bug fix

A colleague adapts the frozen-encoder example from Lesson 2 but reports the logistic head barely trains. Here is their code — spot the bug.

encoder = load_pretrained_checkpoint("esm2_t12_35M")
encoder.train()                      # set to training mode
# (requires_grad left at its default: True, for all parameters)

def embed_sequence(seq):
    tokens = tokenize(seq)
    hidden = encoder(tokens)
    return hidden.mean(dim=1).squeeze(0)

cached_vectors = [embed_sequence(s) for s in labelled_seqs]
head = LogisticRegression(in_dim=480)
head.fit(cached_vectors, labels)
  1. The bug is in LogisticRegression: it should use a linear activation, not logistic.
  2. The bug is that encoder.eval() was never called and the encoder's parameters were never frozen with requires_grad = False, and the forward pass isn't wrapped in a no-grad context — so this isn't a frozen-feature extraction at all.
  3. The bug is that mean(dim=1) should be sum(dim=1) to pool residue embeddings correctly.
  4. The bug is that 400 examples is too few to ever train any model, regardless of setup.
Show answer

Answer: B. Lesson 2's version explicitly calls encoder.eval(), sets every parameter's requires_grad = False, and wraps the forward pass in a no-grad context. Without those three things, this is not linear probing at all — it's an (accidental, incomplete) attempt at full fine-tuning, with the encoder left in training mode and gradient tracking on, which changes memory cost and behavior (e.g. dropout stays active) even though the head still nominally "trains."

A and C are red herrings about naming and pooling choice that don't change whether the encoder is actually frozen. D is false in general — Lesson 2's entire point is that 400 examples is enough precisely because only a small head, not the encoder, is being fit.
